### Template A — "How to get first customers / nobody trusts me yet" threads

> [CUSTOMIZE: Acknowledge their specific situation]
>
> When you have no track record, borrow credibility from the work you've already done:
>
> 1. Do one project (free or discounted) for someone who'll say something real about it
> 2. Get it in writing — one paragraph, their real name
> 3. Put it on your site *before* you talk to the next prospect
>
> That first testimonial breaks the chicken-and-egg. After that, ask every client at job completion — it's the moment they're happiest and most likely to say yes.
>
> Timing: ask the day you deliver, not a week later. Makes a huge difference in completion rate.
>
> For the collection itself I built a simple free tool (socialproof.dev) — a link they fill out in 60 seconds, you approve, you embed. But even "just reply to this email with 2 sentences" works when you nail the timing.

---

### Template B — "Landing page conversion / nobody converts" threads

> [CUSTOMIZE: Engage with something specific from the thread first]
>
> Three things that tend to move conversion more than copy rewrites:
>
> 1. **Testimonials near the CTA** — not on a dedicated reviews page. Most landing pages bury their best social proof. Move your strongest quote above the fold or right next to your "Contact us" button.
>
> 2. **Specific names and details** — "Sarah, freelance designer in Denver" converts better than "S.M." — specificity signals realness.
>
> 3. **Outcome language** — "doubled my inquiry rate" > "really professional." The customer's result is the claim, not your quality.
>
> If getting testimonials is the bottleneck, I built a free tool for it: socialproof.dev (I'm the builder). Your customer clicks a link, writes something, you approve it, one script tag embeds it. The friction of collection is usually what blocks people from having testimonials at all.

### Template A — "How do you collect testimonials / references from clients" threads

> [CUSTOMIZE: Reference something specific from the thread]
>
> Timing is the whole game. Ask at delivery — the moment the work is done, not a week later in a follow-up email. They're happiest right now. Every day that passes the willingness drops.
>
> The exact ask that works: "Hey [name], really glad this came together well. Would you write 2-3 sentences about working together that I could put on my site?"
>
> Keep the bar low — not "leave a Google review" (multiple steps, requires a Google account). "Click this link, write two sentences" gets done.
>
> I built a small free tool for this (socialproof.dev — I'm the builder). A shareable link, they fill it out in 60 seconds, you approve it, it embeds on your portfolio. But even asking via text with a simple "reply to this" works when you nail the ask timing.

---

### Template B — "How do I justify higher rates" threads

> [CUSTOMIZE: Engage with specific advice in the thread first]
>
> The best thing you can do to justify higher rates: let your clients justify them for you.
>
> Testimonials that mention specific outcomes convert price-skeptics. Compare:
> - "Great to work with, very professional" → forgettable
> - "Delivered two days early and saved us from a product launch disaster" → closes deals
>
> When you ask for a testimonial, try asking the outcome question: "What was the result of this project for your business?" instead of "How was working with me?" — you'll get outcome language instead of compliment language, and outcome language is what sells your rate.
