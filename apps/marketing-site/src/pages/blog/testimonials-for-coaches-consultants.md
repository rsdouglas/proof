Blog → Testimonials for Coaches & Consultants


If you're a coach or consultant, you already know that **trust is your product**. Clients aren't buying a physical thing — they're betting on your judgment, your experience, and your ability to help them get somewhere. And the primary way prospects evaluate that before they hire you is through what your past clients say about you.


Yet most coaches and consultants have zero testimonials on their website. Or they have one or two that are vague and years old. Or they have great client feedback — in their email inbox, never shared publicly.


This post is about fixing that gap. Here's exactly how to collect compelling testimonials from your clients, what makes a great one, where to put them, and how to do all of it without feeling like you're begging.


## Why coaches and consultants resist asking for testimonials


The reasons are predictable and human:


- **"I don't want to be pushy."** You've already built a relationship with this client. Asking for something feels like it might damage that.
- **"What if they say no?"** That would feel like a rejection — of your work, your relationship, you.
- **"I feel awkward self-promoting."** Many coaches got into this work to help people, not to market themselves.
- **"I'll do it later."** And then later never comes, and the moment of maximum enthusiasm is gone.


Here's the reframe: **asking for a testimonial is not asking for a favor for yourself. It's asking your client to help you help the next person like them.**


When framed that way — and it's genuinely true — most clients are happy to do it. Your best clients want more people to find you. They want other people to get what they got.


## When to ask (timing is everything)


The best time to ask for a testimonial is **at the moment of highest satisfaction** — not at the end of your engagement, but at the moment your client has just experienced a breakthrough or result.


For coaches and consultants, that might be:


- Right after they land a major client or opportunity (if you're a sales coach)
- The session where they finally cracked a problem they'd been stuck on
- When they report back an outcome they achieved ("I just closed $50k in new business this quarter")
- At the end of a cohort or program when energy is high
- After a particularly strong session where they left with clarity


 The worst time: the final invoice. Asking for a testimonial alongside a bill creates an awkward association and often means they're already mentally "done" with the engagement before they respond to your ask.



If you missed the moment of peak enthusiasm, don't wait forever — just ask now. A slightly delayed testimonial beats no testimonial forever.


## The ask: what to say


The ask should be personal, specific, and low-friction. Here are scripts that work:


### In a session (when they've just had a breakthrough)

 Sarah, I love what you just said about finally having clarity on your pricing. That's exactly the kind of shift I hope for with every client. Would you be willing to share something like that as a testimonial? I have a simple link — takes about 2 minutes on your phone. It really helps other coaches find me who are in the same spot you were six months ago.



### Follow-up email (after a great outcome)

 Subject: Quick favor?

Hi [Name],

What you shared in our last call — about finally feeling confident charging your real rate — made my week.

I'm building out my testimonials page and thought of you immediately. If you have 2 minutes, would you be willing to share what the coaching experience has been like for you? Here's a simple form:

[your-socialproof-link]

Totally no pressure — but if you do, it would mean a lot. It helps other [coaches/consultants/business owners] who are exactly where you were find me.

Thank you,
[Your name]


### End of program (group or cohort)

 You've all done incredible work this cohort. As we wrap up, I have one small request: if this program delivered real value for you, would you be willing to share a quick testimonial?

It doesn't need to be long — even a sentence or two about what shifted for you. Here's the link: [your-socialproof-link]

Your words help me reach the next group of people who could benefit from this work.


## What makes a great coaching testimonial


Most testimonials are disappointingly vague. "She's amazing!" or "Highly recommend!" don't help prospective clients understand what they'll actually get. The testimonials that convert are specific — they describe a before, a transformation, and an outcome.



 Weak testimonial
 Strong testimonial


 "James is a fantastic business coach. Highly recommend."
 "Before working with James I was charging $150/hr and constantly undervalued. Six months later I've restructured my offers, raised my rates to $400/hr, and I have a 3-month waitlist."


 "The coaching was exactly what I needed."
 "I'd been avoiding my pricing conversation for two years. In our first session, we identified why and built a framework I could actually use. I raised my rates the following week."


 "Sarah is so insightful and caring."
 "I came to Sarah burned out and ready to quit my business. Three months later I've restructured my client load, set real boundaries, and actually enjoy my work again."




The difference is specificity. To help clients give you specific testimonials, ask specific questions.


## Guide your clients with better questions


Instead of "Would you leave a testimonial?", give clients a prompt that produces specific answers. SocialProof lets you customize the form prompt — here's what works for coaching and consulting:

 What was the specific problem or situation you were dealing with when you first reached out?
What changed for you during our work together?
What's a concrete result or outcome you can point to?


Or more conversationally:

 What would you tell someone who's on the fence about working with me?


That last one is powerful because it's the exact mental state of your prospective clients — and your happy clients know exactly what to say to them.


## Where to put your testimonials


Your testimonials are most powerful close to a decision point. For coaches and consultants, that means:




#### Work with me page


The page where someone decides to book a call or apply. 3–5 specific testimonials here can dramatically improve conversion.




#### Sales page for programs


For courses or cohorts, testimonials from past participants are often more convincing than everything else on the page combined.




#### Proposal documents


For high-ticket consulting, embed 2–3 relevant testimonials in proposals. Prospects who see peers' results are more likely to sign.




#### Email signature


A rating badge or a short pull quote in your email signature builds credibility with every message you send.




#### Discovery call follow-up


After a sales call, send a follow-up email with 2 testimonials from clients with similar challenges to the prospect.




#### LinkedIn profile


Your LinkedIn "Featured" section can include a link to your testimonials page. LinkedIn recommendations are good but you don't own them.

## Handling the awkwardness: a mindset shift


Here's a reframe that many coaches find useful: your future clients are out there right now, searching for help with a problem you know how to solve. They'll find you — or not — based partly on what your past clients say about you.


When you ask for a testimonial, you're not asking for a favor. You're giving your best past clients the opportunity to connect you with the people who most need what you offer. Most clients genuinely want to do that. You just have to ask.

## Frequently asked questions


### What if a client wants to give a testimonial but asks me to write it for them?


It happens — and it's fine, with one condition: send them a draft and make them edit it until it's in their words. A testimonial you write entirely is called copy, not a testimonial. But a ghost-drafted testimonial that the client reads, edits, and approves is genuine — it just needed a push to get there. Ask them to change any words that don't sound like them.


### Should I ask for video testimonials?


Video testimonials are powerful but have much lower completion rates. The ask is bigger, the friction is higher, and most clients won't do it. Text testimonials collected easily are worth more than video testimonials never collected. Start with text, then occasionally ask your most enthusiastic clients for a short video.


### How many testimonials do I need?


There's no magic number. Five strong, specific testimonials will outperform fifty vague ones. Focus on quality and specificity over quantity. That said, having 20+ testimonials signals to prospects that you're well-established and reliably deliver results.


### Should I get testimonials on LinkedIn instead?


LinkedIn recommendations are fine, but you don't own them — if LinkedIn changes or you lose access, they're gone. More importantly, you can't control how they display. Collect testimonials somewhere you own (like SocialProof), then repurpose them wherever you want — including linking from your LinkedIn.


### What about confidentiality? Some clients don't want to be named.


Respect this and offer an opt-out. "First name + industry" is often enough: "Sarah, Business Coach." Some clients will want to remain anonymous entirely — use their testimonial with a note like "Client, executive coaching, 2024." Even anonymous testimonials are better than none.
