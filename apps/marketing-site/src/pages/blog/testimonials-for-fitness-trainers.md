A person hiring a fitness trainer is investing real money in a commitment that requires motivation, consistency, and trust in their coach. They're not just buying sessions — they're buying a belief that change is possible.

Testimonials from clients who made that change under your guidance are the most powerful thing you can put in front of a prospective client.

## Who Is Looking

- People who've failed with gym memberships and want accountability
- Postpartum parents returning to fitness
- Individuals with specific goals (marathon, weight loss, muscle gain, rehab)
- People over 50 looking for age-appropriate training
- Athletes adding strength or conditioning work

## When to Ask

**At the 30-day milestone:**
> "One month in and you're showing up consistently — that's the hardest part. If you're feeling the difference, I'd love a Google review. It helps people who are where you were 30 days ago find the right coach: [link]"

**When a client hits a major goal:**
> "You did it — that was the goal you came in with. If this experience is something you'd want others to know about, a review would mean everything: [link]"

## What a Strong Fitness Trainer Review Looks Like

*"I tried three gyms and two apps before I found [trainer's name]. In 6 months I lost 28 pounds, but more than that — I stopped dreading exercise. She adapts every session to how I'm feeling, pushes me when I need it, and talks me off the ledge when I want to quit. I've referred four coworkers and they've all had the same experience. This is the first time fitness has actually stuck."*

Strong elements: social proof of prior failures, specific outcome (28 lbs), behavioral change (no longer dreading exercise), personalization, referral chain, and sustainability framing.
