Parents don't buy sleep consulting. They buy sleep. They buy survival. They buy being able to function as a human being again.

If you're a sleep consultant, you're selling relief — and testimonials are the only way to make that relief feel real to a parent who hasn't experienced it yet.

## Who Is Looking for a Sleep Consultant

- Exhausted parents of 4-month to 3-year-olds who aren't sleeping through the night
- Parents who've tried everything (cry it out, Ferber, shushing, nursing to sleep) and nothing has worked
- Parents hesitant to do sleep training and looking for a gentle approach
- Parents of multiples, with complex schedules
- Postpartum parents dealing with sleep deprivation affecting mental health
- Toddler parents dealing with bedtime battles and early waking

## When to Ask

**3 nights after the training ends (when results are visible):**
> "How did last night go? If you're sleeping better, would you mind sharing a few sentences about your experience? It would mean everything to another family where you were a few weeks ago: [link]"

**At 2-week check-in:**
> "Two weeks in — how's it going? If the program has made a difference, I'd love for you to share it. It helps other exhausted parents find help: [link]"

## What a Strong Sleep Consultant Testimonial Looks Like

*"We were waking up 5 to 8 times per night with our 9-month-old. We'd tried everything, and I was starting to fall apart. After one week with [name]'s gentle sleep plan, our son was sleeping 11 hours straight. [Name] was available every single day to answer questions and adjust the plan based on how our son responded. The investment paid for itself the first night I slept more than 3 hours. I've recommended her to every new parent I know."*

Strong elements: specific suffering context (8 wake-ups, "falling apart"), baby's age, named approach (gentle), specific outcome (11 hours), timeline (one week), support quality (available daily, personalized), ROI framing, and strong referral.

## The Before/After Is Your Product

Sleep consulting testimonials should almost always include a before-and-after structure:
- **Before:** How many wake-ups? How old was the child? How long had this been going on? What had they tried?
- **After:** Specific sleep duration. Parent's emotional state. How quickly it happened.

Prompt clients with this directly:
> "Can you describe what nights looked like before we started, and what they look like now?"
