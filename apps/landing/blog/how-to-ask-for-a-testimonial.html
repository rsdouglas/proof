How to Ask for a Testimonial: 7 Email Scripts That Actually Work

Most business owners never ask. The ones who do usually ask wrong. Here are 7 copy-paste scripts — with timing tips that double response rates.

Here's an uncomfortable truth: your happy customers are not going to leave you a testimonial unprompted. They're busy. They've moved on. They meant to do it, but life happened.

The businesses with walls of glowing testimonials got them because someone asked. Usually more than once. In a way that made it easy to say yes.

This guide gives you the scripts. But first — let's fix the mindset that stops most people from asking in the first place.

Why asking for testimonials feels so hard

The most common reason business owners don't ask for testimonials: it feels like asking for a favor. Like you're burdening someone. Like it's a little bit desperate.

Flip that around. If someone gave you genuinely good work — designed your logo, fixed your plumbing, helped you lose 20 pounds — and they asked you to spend two minutes telling them what you thought, would you feel burdened? Or would you feel glad you had a chance to say thanks?

Your happy customers want to help you. They just need a nudge and a clear path to do it. That's all you're providing.

The reframe: Asking for a testimonial isn't asking for a favor. It's giving your customer a chance to do something they already want to do.

When to ask (timing is everything)

The biggest factor in testimonial response rates isn't your script — it's when you send it. The best times:

  • Right after a win. A project just wrapped. A goal was hit. Results just came in. This is the peak of their positive feeling — catch it here.
  • After a compliment. Someone emails "this is incredible" or "you've totally changed how we do X." Reply: "I'm so glad — would you mind putting that in writing? It would mean a lot."
  • At the 30-day mark. For service businesses, one month in is when clients have seen enough results to speak credibly but haven't forgotten the journey.
  • After a milestone. First sale, first 100 subscribers, first successful event — any moment your customer reaches a goal with your help.

The worst time to ask: randomly, months after the project, when the client has mentally moved on. Even if they were thrilled, recalling and articulating that feeling takes real effort. Don't make them work that hard.

7 email scripts you can use today

These scripts are short. Deliberately. The longer your ask, the lower your response rate. Give them one thing to do, make it easy, and get out of the way.

Script 1: The simple ask (works for almost anything)

Use when: a project just finished or a customer just had a great experience.

Email script

Subject: Quick question before I let you go

Hi [Name],

Really glad [project/product/service] went well — it was a pleasure working with you.

One quick ask: would you be willing to write a short testimonial about your experience? Even two or three sentences about what was helpful would mean a lot, and it helps other [clients/customers/people] like you find me.

You can leave it here: [your Vouch link]

No pressure at all — just thought I'd ask while things are fresh!

Thanks either way,
[Your name]

Script 2: The reply-to-a-compliment

Use when: someone just said something nice over email, Slack, or text.

Email script

Subject: Re: [their compliment subject line]

That genuinely made my day — thank you so much for saying that.

I know this is a lot to ask, but would you be willing to put something like that in writing? I'm building up testimonials for my site, and hearing it from someone like you would carry a lot of weight.

Takes less than 2 minutes: [your Vouch link]

Either way, I really appreciate the kind words. 🙏

[Your name]

Pro tip: This is the highest-converting script of the seven because the customer is already in a positive emotional state. Don't wait — reply to compliments within the hour.

Script 3: The specific-question ask

Use when: you want to steer the testimonial toward a specific angle (results, ease of working together, a particular feature).

Email script

Subject: A quick favor?

Hi [Name],

I'm putting together testimonials from clients and thought of you — you've been a pleasure to work with and I'd love to share your experience.

If you're willing, could you answer just one question in a few sentences:

"What was the biggest result you saw after working with me / using [product]?"

You can type your answer here (takes 2 minutes): [your Vouch link]

Thank you so much!

[Your name]

Script 4: The Shopify / e-commerce post-purchase

Use when: you run an online store and want testimonials about products, shipping, or overall experience.

Email script

Subject: How's the [product] treating you?

Hi [Name],

Your order arrived a couple weeks ago — I hope you're loving the [product]!

If you have 60 seconds, I'd love to know what you think. A quick testimonial helps other shoppers feel confident buying, and it helps a small business like ours grow.

Share your experience here: [your Vouch link]

No pressure — but we really do read every single one.

Thanks for being a customer,
[Your name / store name]

Script 5: The freelancer / agency wrap-up

Use when: you've just delivered a project (design, writing, development, consulting).

Email script

Subject: Final files + one small ask

Hi [Name],

Here are the final deliverables — [brief description]. Everything is yours to use however you'd like.

It's been a great project and I hope the results speak for themselves. I'd love to stay in touch for future work.

One last thing: if you're happy with how it went, a short testimonial would mean a lot to me. Other clients love hearing from people who've been through the process — what it was like, what the outcome was, anything honest.

[your Vouch link] — takes about 2 minutes.

Thank you for trusting me with this. Genuinely enjoyed it.

[Your name]

Script 6: The coach / consultant check-in

Use when: you work with clients over time and want to capture testimonials at a natural milestone.

Email script

Subject: Checking in — and a small ask

Hi [Name],

I was thinking about you this week — how are things going with [goal/area you worked on together]?

We've come a long way since [starting point]. If you feel like your experience working together has been worth it, I'd love to hear it in your own words — it helps other [people in their situation] find me and decide if we're a good fit.

No pressure, and no format required — just honest words: [your Vouch link]

Either way, I'm rooting for you.

Make it easy to say yes

The scripts above all share one thing: they link directly to a testimonial form. Not "reply to this email." Not "leave a Google review." A single link that takes them exactly where they need to go.

This matters more than you think. Every step you add — "go to Google, search for my business, click Reviews, click Write a Review" — cuts your response rate in half. A single link that opens a short form is the difference between 5% response and 25%.

The form itself should be simple:

  • Name and photo (optional — but photos make testimonials 3x more trustworthy)
  • One or two open questions, not ten
  • Mobile-friendly — most people will open the link on their phone
  • Confirmation that you'll ask before publishing anything

What to do if they don't respond

Send one follow-up. Just one. Wait 5–7 days, then:

Follow-up script

Subject: Re: [original subject]

Hi [Name] — just bumping this up in case it got buried! No pressure at all, just wanted to make sure you saw it.

[your Vouch link]

[Your name]

That's it. If they don't reply to the follow-up, let it go. Chasing a testimonial creates awkwardness that damages the relationship — and you need to preserve that relationship for repeat business and referrals, which are worth more than any testimonial.

Volume math: If you send 20 testimonial requests and get a 20% response rate, that's 4 testimonials. Send them to 50 past clients: 10 testimonials. The scripts matter less than the number of people you actually ask.

Where to put testimonials once you have them

Getting testimonials is step one. The second step — which most business owners skip — is actually displaying them somewhere visitors will see them.

The highest-impact placements:

  • Above the fold on your homepage. This is prime real estate. A rotating testimonial widget right under your headline does more for conversions than almost anything else.
  • Next to your pricing or "book now" button. Anxiety peaks right before someone commits. A testimonial right there can push them over the line.
  • On a dedicated testimonials page. For service businesses, prospects often search "[your name] reviews" — give them a page to land on.
  • In your email signature. "See what clients say →" with a link. Subtle, always-on social proof.
